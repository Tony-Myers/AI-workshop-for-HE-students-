Why Ethics Matters

Beyond the rules of academic integrity, using AI ethically means understanding its real-world impact. As a future professional, you must be aware of the key ethical challenges associated with AI, including data privacy, algorithmic bias, and accountability.

Pillar 1: Data Privacy & Security

When you use a public AI tool (like the free version of ChatGPT), your prompts can be saved, reviewed by humans, and even used to train future versions of the model. This creates a significant risk.

Professional & Ethical Warning

You must NEVER paste sensitive, confidential, or personally identifiable information into a public AI tool. This includes:

  • Research data containing participant information.
  • Confidential information from a placement or workplace.
  • Personal health information about yourself or anyone else.
  • Unpublished research or intellectual property.

Doing so is a serious data breach and a violation of professional ethics.

Why Our University Tools are Safer

The tools we recommend, like the Microsoft Copilot available through your university account, have enhanced data protection. Your prompts are not saved for training the public model. This is why we call this a "walled garden" approach—it's a safer environment for your academic work. However, you should still exercise caution and avoid inputting highly sensitive personal data.

Pillar 2: Algorithmic Bias ("Bias In, Bias Out")

AI models are trained on vast amounts of text and images from the internet. This data, created by humans, is full of human biases, stereotypes, and historical inequalities.

The AI learns these patterns. This is what "Bias In, Bias Out" means. If the data an AI trained on under-represents women in leadership roles or associates certain sports with specific genders or races, the AI's "knowledge" will reflect and even amplify these biases.

Try It Yourself

Go to an AI image generator (like in Microsoft Copilot). Try these prompts and see what you get:

  • "A picture of a successful CEO"
  • "A group of nurses"
  • "A fast runner"
  • "A photo of a sports scientist"

Does the output look diverse? Does it challenge or reinforce stereotypes? This is bias in action. As a sport professional, you must be critical of AI outputs that may reinforce harmful stereotypes about athletes, body types, or abilities.

Pillar 3: Intellectual Property & Ownership

The law is still catching up with AI. It is not clear who "owns" AI-generated content. You cannot copyright content created solely by an AI.

This is another reason why your personal contribution, critical evaluation, and modification are essential. Your academic work must be *your* work. AI can be a collaborator (at Level 5) or a tool (Levels 2-4), but you are the author and owner of the final submission.

Pillar 4: Accountability & Hallucinations

As we covered in the understanding.html guide, AI tools can "hallucinate"—make up facts, statistics, and even academic references.

You Are Accountable

If you use AI at Level 4 (Content Generation) and your final submission contains a fake statistic or a non-existent reference that the AI produced, you are 100% responsible for that error.

"The AI told me" is not a valid excuse. It is the academic equivalent of "I found it on Wikipedia." Your job as a university student is to critically verify all information. This is why Level 4 requires critical evaluation and substantial modification.
