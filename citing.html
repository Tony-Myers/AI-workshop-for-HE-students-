Why, What, and When to Cite AI

Using AI in your work requires transparency. This is done in two ways: documentation (for your marker) and citation (for your reader). They are not the same thing.

Documentation vs. Citation

  • Documentation (The AI Evidence Form): This is an assessment requirement. It's a detailed log of your *process* (prompts, verification, changes) that you submit alongside your work. This shows your marker *how* you used AI and proves your personal contribution.
  • Citation (In-text & Reference List): This is an academic requirement. It formally attributes any specific ideas, text, or images from AI that are in your final work. This tells your *reader* where the information came true, just like citing a book or journal.

Guideline 1: Your Module Brief is Law

You can ONLY use AI at the level specified in your assignment brief. If your brief says Level 1 (No AI), you cannot use it at all. If it says Level 3 (Editing), you must not use AI to generate content (Level 4).

Properly citing Level 4 use on a Level 3 assignment is still academic misconduct. This guide only applies when your brief *permits* you to use AI at a level that requires citation (typically Levels 4-6).

How to Cite AI-Generated Content (e.g., APA 7th Edition)

Most major citation styles (APA, MLA, Chicago) now have guidelines for citing AI. Always check the latest guidance from the official style guides. The general principle is to treat the AI tool as the "author."

For APA 7th Edition, the guidance is to cite the creator of the model as the "author."

Example: Citing AI-Generated Text (Levels 4-6)

If you ask an AI to generate text that you then adapt, verify, and include in your work, you must cite it.

Reference List Entry: 
OpenAI. (2023). *ChatGPT* (Mar 14 version) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com/
In-Text Citation:

When prompted, the AI model suggested that the primary components of a motor skill acquisition programme should include "...." (OpenAI, 2023).

Alternatively, you can integrate the citation into your text:

OpenAI's (2023) ChatGPT platform provided an initial summary of the key debates in...

Example: Citing AI-Generated Images

If you use an AI to generate an image for a presentation or report (and it is permitted), you must cite it in the figure caption.

Figure Caption:

Figure 1. A diagram illustrating the phases of the stretch-shortening cycle.

Note. Image generated using Microsoft Copilot (2024).

How to Acknowledge AI Use (Levels 2-3)

What if you only used AI for idea generation (Level 2) or editing (Level 3)? This usually doesn't require a formal citation, but for larger projects (like a dissertation), it's good practice to include a note in your 'Acknowledgements' or 'Methods' section.

This provides transparency without cluttering your reference list.

Example Acknowledgement: 
I would like to acknowledge the use of generative AI (Microsoft Copilot) in this project. Specifically, it was used at Level 3 to improve the clarity and grammar of my initial draft, as permitted by the module guidelines. All critical analysis and content remain my own.

The Golden Rule: When in Doubt, Ask

AI citation is a new and evolving field. The rules may differ by discipline and even by assignment. Always check your module brief and, if you are unsure, ask your lecturer for guidance.
