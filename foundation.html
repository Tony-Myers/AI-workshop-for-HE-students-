Module 1: Understanding the Framework & Academic Integrity

Essential Foundation

Before using AI for any assessment, you must understand Birmingham Newman University's 6-level framework and what constitutes academic misconduct.

The 6-Level Framework

  • Level 1: No AI - You complete the assessment entirely on your own
  • Level 2: Idea Generation - AI helps you brainstorm but you write everything yourself
  • Level 3: Editing - AI helps improve your writing, but you must include your original work
  • Level 4: Task Completion - AI does some tasks, you evaluate and discuss the outputs
  • Level 5: Collaboration - AI works alongside you throughout
  • Level 6: Mandatory - You must use AI and document everything you did
⚠️ Critical: Always check your assignment brief to see which level applies. Using AI at the wrong level can result in academic misconduct proceedings.

What Is Academic Misconduct with AI?

Academic misconduct occurs when you:

  • Use AI when the assignment specifies "Level 1: No AI"
  • Submit AI-generated content as your own work (when not at Level 4, 5, or 6)
  • Fail to cite AI use when required (Levels 4, 5, 6)
  • Use AI beyond what the level permits (e.g., having AI write content at Level 2)
  • Fail to provide required documentation (e.g., AI Evidence Form for SPU412)

Activity 1.1: Test Your Understanding

Scenario Practice

Your assignment brief states "Level 1: No AI". Can you:

  • Use AI to help you understand a concept? (No - this is still AI assistance)
  • Use spell check in Microsoft Word? (Yes - basic tools are allowed)
  • Ask ChatGPT to explain biomechanics terminology? (No - Level 1 means no AI at all)

Key point: Level 1 means you must complete the work using only your own knowledge and non-AI tools.

Module 3: Level 1 - When AI Cannot Be Used

Level 1

Some assessments require you to complete work entirely without AI. This is important for demonstrating your own knowledge and skills.

Why Level 1 Exists

Level 1 assessments ensure that:

  • You can demonstrate understanding without AI assistance
  • Your work reflects your personal knowledge and capabilities
  • You develop essential skills that AI cannot replace
  • Assessment is fair and accurately measures your learning

Common Level 1 Assessments in Sport Science

  • In-class exams and tests
  • Practical assessments (coaching sessions, sports therapy demonstrations)
  • Some calculations and data analysis (where you must show your working)
  • Reflections on placement or practical experiences (your own authentic voice)

Sport Science Example: Biomechanics Practical

Scenario: You are assessed on identifying muscle attachments and actions on a skeleton model.

Level 1 approach:

  • Study using textbooks and lecture notes
  • Practice with physical models or diagrams
  • Test yourself without AI assistance
  • Demonstrate your knowledge in the practical exam

Why AI cannot help here: The assessment measures your ability to physically identify structures and recall information without external aids.

What You CAN Use at Level 1

  • Your lecture notes and textbooks
  • Basic spell check and grammar check in Word
  • Calculators for numerical work (unless specifically prohibited)
  • Library resources and journal articles
  • Discussions with tutors and peers (for understanding, not for completing work)

Module 4: Level 2 - AI for Idea Generation

Level 2

At Level 2, you can use AI to help brainstorm ideas, create outlines, and structure your thinking. However, all actual content in your submission must be your own words.

What Level 2 Allows

  • Brainstorming: Generating initial ideas for essays or presentations
  • Structuring: Creating outlines or frameworks for your work
  • Exploring topics: Identifying different angles or perspectives
  • Planning: Developing a logical flow for your arguments

What Level 2 Does NOT Allow

  • Having AI write any part of your final submission
  • Using AI-generated sentences or paragraphs in your work
  • Copying AI suggestions directly into your document
  • Using AI to improve or edit your actual writing
Key Rule: No AI content can appear in your final submission. Everything submitted must be in your own words.

Sport Science Example: Essay on Training Adaptations

Task: Write a 2000-word essay on "Physiological adaptations to endurance training"

Level 2 AI Use (Allowed):

Prompt to Copilot: "Help me brainstorm different sections I could include in an essay about physiological adaptations to endurance training"

AI Response might include:

  • Cardiovascular adaptations
  • Respiratory system changes
  • Muscular adaptations
  • Metabolic changes
  • Neural adaptations

What you do next:

  • Use this list to guide your research
  • Read academic sources on each topic
  • Write the entire essay in your own words
  • Do not include any AI-generated text in your submission

Activity 4.1: Practice Level 2 Use

Try using the Socratic Tutor to explore a topic:

  1. Open Microsoft Copilot and select the Socratic Tutor agent
  2. Enter: "I need to write about plyometric training. What are the main areas I should cover?"
  3. Note the suggestions but do not copy any text
  4. Use the ideas to guide your own research and writing

Good Prompts for Level 2

  • "What are the main arguments for and against [topic]?"
  • "Help me create an outline for an essay on [topic]"
  • "What different perspectives exist on [topic]?"
  • "Suggest a logical structure for presenting information about [topic]"
  • "What questions should I consider when researching [topic]?"

Module 5: Level 3 - AI for Editing

Level 3

At Level 3, you can use AI to improve the clarity and quality of your own writing. However, you must submit both your original work (in an appendix) and the AI-edited version.

What Level 3 Allows

  • Clarity improvements: Making sentences clearer and more concise
  • Grammar and spelling: Fixing errors and improving flow
  • Readability: Adjusting language for better comprehension
  • Structure refinement: Improving paragraph transitions

What Level 3 Does NOT Allow

  • AI generating new content or ideas
  • AI adding information you did not write
  • AI changing the meaning or arguments of your work
  • Failing to include your original work in the appendix
Critical Requirement: You MUST include your original, unedited work in an appendix. Without this, you cannot demonstrate that the work is fundamentally yours.

Sport Science Example: Lab Report Editing

Your Original Writing:

"In the experiment we did we found that when people do exercise their heart rate goes up a lot and it stays up for a while after they stop and this is because the body needs more oxygen when exercising."

Prompt to Copilot:

"Please improve the clarity of this sentence while keeping all the same information and meaning."

AI-Edited Version (Example):

"The experiment demonstrated that exercise significantly increases heart rate, which remains elevated post-exercise due to the body's increased oxygen demand during physical activity."

What you submit:

  • Main document: The AI-edited version
  • Appendix: Your original, unedited writing

Activity 5.1: Practice Level 3 Editing

  1. Write a short paragraph about a sport science concept in your own words
  2. Save this as your "original version"
  3. Use Microsoft Copilot to improve clarity: "Please improve the clarity and flow of this text while keeping the same meaning: [paste your text]"
  4. Compare the original and edited versions
  5. Ensure the meaning has not changed

Good Prompts for Level 3

  • "Improve the clarity of this text while maintaining the same meaning"
  • "Fix grammar and spelling errors in this paragraph"
  • "Make this text more concise without losing information"
  • "Improve the academic tone of this writing"
  • "Suggest better transitions between these paragraphs"

Module 6: Finding Sport Science Sources

Research Skills

Whether or not you use AI, you need to find high-quality academic sources for your work. This module teaches you how to use library resources effectively.

Using SPORTDiscus

SPORTDiscus is the main database for sport science research. Access it through the library A-Z databases.

  1. Go to the Newman Library website
  2. Click "Databases A-Z"
  3. Find and click "SPORTDiscus"
  4. Log in with your university credentials
  5. Use the search box to find articles

Effective Search Strategies

  • Use specific terms: Instead of "running", try "aerobic exercise" or "endurance training"
  • Use AND to combine concepts: "plyometric training AND injury prevention"
  • Use OR for similar terms: "strength training OR resistance training"
  • Use quotation marks for exact phrases: "interval training"
  • Limit to peer-reviewed: Check the "peer-reviewed" box to get quality sources

Sport Science Search Example

Research question: How does high-intensity interval training affect cardiovascular fitness in young adults?

Search terms to try:

  • "high-intensity interval training" AND "cardiovascular fitness"
  • HIIT AND "aerobic capacity"
  • "interval training" AND VO2max AND adults

Evaluating Source Quality

Not all sources are equal. Ask yourself:

  • Is it peer-reviewed?
  • Is it recent (within last 5-10 years)?
  • Are the authors experts in sport science?
  • Does it have clear methodology and results?
  • Is it published in a reputable journal?

Activity 6.1: Find Three Quality Sources

  1. Choose a sport science topic you are studying
  2. Log in to SPORTDiscus
  3. Search for peer-reviewed articles from the last 5 years
  4. Find three articles relevant to your topic
  5. Save the citation details using the referencing guide

Module 7: Understanding NotebookLM

Essential Tool

NotebookLM is a "source-grounded" AI tool that only uses documents you upload. This makes it safer and more reliable than general AI tools for academic work, but not infallible.

Why NotebookLM Is Different

  • Source-grounded: Only uses information from your uploaded documents (but still check what it has claimed by using the links provided to source material)
  • Citations provided: Shows you exactly where information came from (check these support the claims made in the summary)
  • Less likely to "hallucinate": Will not usually make up facts not in your sources, but do check particularly if you have multiple documents uploaded
  • Audio summaries: Can create podcast-style overviews

Activity 7.1: Your First NotebookLM Experience

  1. Go to notebooklm.google.com and sign in
  2. Click "+ New notebook"
  3. Click "+ Add Source" and upload a PDF (use a journal article from SPORTDiscus or lecture notes)
  4. Once uploaded, click on the document name to open it
  5. In the chat box at the bottom, type: "Generate a 5-point summary of this source"
  6. Read the summary and click on the [1] [2] [3] citation numbers to see where the information came from
  7. Try asking: "Create a 2-minute audio overview of this source"
  8. Listen to the audio summary

Sport Science Example: Understanding a Research Article

Scenario: You have found a complex research article on "Effects of creatine supplementation on muscle performance"

How NotebookLM can help:

  1. Upload the article PDF to NotebookLM
  2. Ask: "What were the main findings of this study?"
  3. Ask: "What methodology did the researchers use?"
  4. Ask: "What are the limitations mentioned?"
  5. Use the responses to guide your deeper reading

Remember: NotebookLM helps you understand sources, but you still need to read and cite the original article in your work.

Good NotebookLM Prompts for Sport Science

  • "What is the main research question in this study?"
  • "Summarise the methodology used"
  • "What were the key results?"
  • "What conclusions did the authors draw?"
  • "What are the practical applications mentioned?"
  • "Compare the findings in these two sources" (if you have uploaded multiple documents)

Module 8: Basic Fact-Checking Skills

Critical Skill

AI tools can make mistakes. You must always verify information before using it in your work.

Why Fact-Checking Matters

  • AI can "hallucinate" (make up information that sounds plausible but is false)
  • AI might provide outdated information
  • AI can misinterpret complex topics
  • You are responsible for the accuracy of your work, not the AI

How to Fact-Check AI Outputs

  1. Check against academic sources: Search SPORTDiscus or Google Scholar for the claim
  2. Look for citations: Does the AI provide sources? (NotebookLM does, general Copilot often does not)
  3. Cross-reference: Do multiple reliable sources agree?
  4. Ask the Academic Rigour Assistant: This agent is designed to provide evidence-based answers
  5. Consult your lecturer: If unsure, ask for guidance

Fact-Checking Example

AI claim: "Plyometric training increases muscle strength by 30-40% in 6 weeks"

Fact-checking process:

  1. Ask: "Does this sound realistic?" (30-40% in 6 weeks seems high)
  2. Search SPORTDiscus: "plyometric training strength gains"
  3. Read actual research studies
  4. Discovery: Gains vary widely (5-20% is more typical) depending on training status, programme design, and measurement methods
  5. Conclusion: The AI claim is oversimplified and potentially misleading

Red Flags: When to Be Extra Cautious

  • Specific statistics or percentages without sources
  • Claims that seem too good to be true
  • Information about very recent events or research
  • Medical or health advice
  • Anything that contradicts your lecture notes or textbooks

Activity 8.1: Practice Fact-Checking

  1. Ask Microsoft Copilot a sport science question
  2. Note any specific claims or statistics in the response
  3. Search SPORTDiscus for research on the same topic
  4. Compare: Does the research support what the AI said?
  5. If there are discrepancies, which source is more reliable?

Practical Application

Now that you understand the basics, let us apply these skills to your actual assessments.

For SPU412: Personal Development Presentation

SPU412 uses Levels 2-3 and requires an AI Evidence Form.

  • Required: Complete the AI Evidence Form documenting your use

For Other Level 1 Assessments

Remember: Some assessments prohibit all AI use. Always check your brief.

  • Practical exams (coaching, sports therapy)
  • In-class tests
  • Some lab reports (check with your lecturer)

Decision Tree: Can I Use AI?

  1. Check your assignment brief: What level does it specify?
  2. Level 1? → No AI allowed at all
  3. Level 2? → AI for ideas only, no AI text in submission
  4. Level 3? → AI for editing, must include original in appendix
  5. Still unsure? → Ask your lecturer BEFORE using AI
