Part 1: Core Competency Review (Quick Check)

Before diving into advanced challenges, ensure you are comfortable with the core tools and concepts. Complete these tasks efficiently to establish your baseline.

Task 1: Image Generation & Bias Evaluation

Action: Use Microsoft Copilot to generate an image relevant to your field (e.g., "a sports psychology consultation", "a biomechanics laboratory", "a nutrition counselling session").

Critical Analysis:

  • What stereotypes (gender, race, age, body type) are embedded in the default outputs?
  • How many iterations are needed to achieve a professionally appropriate, diverse, UK-contextualised image?
  • What does this reveal about AI training data and bias?

Reflection: Consider the implications for using AI-generated images in professional practice or educational materials.

Task 2: NotebookLM Advanced Analysis

Action: Upload 3-4 research articles on the same sport science topic to NotebookLM.

Advanced Prompts:

  • "Compare the methodological approaches across these studies"
  • "Identify areas of agreement and disagreement between authors"
  • "What limitations are acknowledged in each study?"
  • "What research gaps emerge from these sources?"

Critical Analysis:

  • How accurate is NotebookLM's synthesis compared to your own reading?
  • What nuances does it miss?
  • How reliable are its citations?

Task 3: Academic Rigour Assistant Evaluation

Action: Ask the Academic Rigour Assistant a complex question from your discipline.

Example prompts:

  • "What is the current evidence for mental imagery enhancing motor skill acquisition?"
  • "How effective is blood flow restriction training for muscle hypertrophy?"
  • "What are the mechanisms underlying post-activation potentiation?"

Critical Task:

  1. Note the Academic Rigour Assistant's response
  2. Search SPORTDiscus for recent systematic reviews or meta-analyses on the topic
  3. Compare: Where did the AI accurately represent the evidence? Where did it oversimplify?
  4. Identify any claims requiring correction or nuance

Task 4: Socratic Tutor Stress Test

Action: Use the Socratic Tutor to explore a complex concept, but intentionally provide incorrect statements to see how it responds.

Test scenarios:

  • "I think lactate causes muscle fatigue during exercise" (common misconception)
  • "Static stretching always improves performance" (oversimplification)
  • "Carbohydrate loading is only useful for marathons" (too narrow)

Critical Analysis:

  • Does the Socratic Tutor effectively correct misconceptions?
  • Does it guide you to accurate understanding through questioning?
  • When does the Socratic method become frustrating rather than helpful?
  • How would you recommend students use this tool effectively?

Part 2: Advanced Challenges

These challenges require disciplinary expertise, critical thinking, and the ability to evaluate AI tools at a sophisticated level. Choose challenges relevant to your interests and programme requirements.

Challenge A: Comprehensive Tool Evaluation

Critical Analysis

Objective: Systematically compare institutional AI tools with alternatives to inform evidence-based recommendations.

Task:

  1. Select ONE of our institutional tools (NotebookLM or Academic Rigour Assistant)
  2. Identify a comparable alternative:
    • For NotebookLM: ChatGPT with document upload, Claude Projects, or Perplexity
    • For Academic Rigour Assistant: Standard ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini
  3. Design test cases specific to sport science (e.g., analysing research on training adaptations)
  4. Compare systematically:
    • Accuracy and completeness of responses
    • Citation quality and traceability
    • Handling of conflicting evidence
    • Recognition of uncertainty and limitations
    • Ease of use for undergraduate students
    • Risk of hallucination or bias

Deliverable: A 2-3 page comparative analysis with specific examples demonstrating strengths and weaknesses of each tool. Include recommendations for:

  • Which tool for which purpose
  • Specific warnings students must receive
  • Recommended guardrails or usage policies

Challenge B: Design a Discipline-Specific AI Workflow

Applied Design

Objective: Create a validated AI-assisted workflow for a specific sport science task.

Choose one scenario:

  • Biomechanics: Analysing movement patterns from video data
  • Exercise Physiology: Designing periodised training programmes
  • Sports Psychology: Developing mental skills training interventions
  • Sports Therapy: Creating injury rehabilitation protocols
  • Sport Nutrition: Planning athlete nutrition strategies

Task:

  1. Design a step-by-step workflow showing:
    • Which AI tools to use at each stage
    • What human expertise is essential (cannot be delegated to AI)
    • How to verify AI outputs against professional standards
    • What safety checks are required
  2. Test the workflow with a real case or scenario
  3. Identify failure points:
    • Where did AI perform poorly?
    • What corrections were necessary?
    • What additional verification was needed?
  4. Refine the workflow based on testing

Deliverable: A documented workflow (flowchart or detailed written procedure) with:

  • Step-by-step instructions
  • Evidence from your testing
  • Identified limitations and risks
  • Recommendations for safe implementation

Challenge C: Assessment Design with AI Integration

Pedagogical Innovation

Objective: Design a learning activity that uses AI productively while maintaining academic integrity and assessing genuine learning.

Task:

  1. Choose either NotebookLM or the Socratic Tutor
  2. Design a complete learning activity including:
    • Learning objective: What specific skill or knowledge will students develop?
    • Pre-activity briefing: What do students need to know before starting?
    • Structured task: Step-by-step instructions for students
    • Assessment integration: How will you verify learning occurred? What evidence will students produce?
    • AI containment strategy: How do you prevent passive consumption or plagiarism?
  3. Apply the "Goldilocks principle": The AI should be useful enough to enhance learning but constrained enough that students cannot complete the assessment by copying outputs

Examples to inspire you:

  • NotebookLM activity: Students upload three conflicting expert sources, use NotebookLM to identify points of disagreement, then without AI write an analysis explaining which expert is most persuasive and why
  • Socratic Tutor activity: Students screenshot their dialogue working through a problem, then submit a reflective annotation explaining where the Socratic questioning helped, where it hindered, and what they learned about their own thinking process

Deliverable: A one-page activity plan suitable for sharing with programme teams, including learning objectives, student instructions, and assessment criteria.

Challenge D: Ethical Analysis of AI in Sport Science

Professional Ethics

Objective: Critically examine the ethical implications of AI use in sport science professional practice.

Task:

  1. Consider a realistic scenario:
    • Using AI to generate training programmes for clients
    • Using AI to analyse injury risk factors
    • Using AI to provide nutrition advice
    • Using AI to develop mental skills interventions
  2. Analyse from multiple ethical perspectives:
    • Professional responsibility: What duty of care do practitioners owe clients?
    • Competence: When does AI use exceed practitioner competence?
    • Transparency: Should clients be informed about AI use? How?
    • Liability: Who is responsible when AI recommendations cause harm?
    • Evidence base: How do practitioners verify AI claims are evidence-based?
  3. Research professional body guidance (e.g., BASES, HCPC, REPS)
  4. Develop ethical guidelines for your scenario

Deliverable: A 2-3 page ethical analysis including:

  • Identification of key ethical issues
  • Reference to professional standards
  • Proposed guidelines for ethical AI use
  • Recommendations for professional practice

Challenge E: AI Limitations Research Project

Empirical Investigation

Objective: Systematically document where AI tools fail or produce problematic outputs in sport science contexts.

Task:

  1. Select a specific sport science subdomain (e.g., resistance training, sports nutrition, injury prevention)
  2. Design test questions covering:
    • Basic factual knowledge
    • Complex physiological mechanisms
    • Applied problem-solving
    • Current controversies or debates
  3. Test multiple AI tools (at least 3) with identical questions
  4. Evaluate responses against academic literature:
    • Factual accuracy
    • Completeness
    • Nuance and qualification
    • Recognition of uncertainty
    • Handling of conflicting evidence
  5. Document patterns of failure:
    • Types of questions AI handles poorly
    • Common errors or misconceptions
    • Hallucinations or fabricated citations
    • Oversimplifications of complex topics

Deliverable: A systematic report (3-5 pages) documenting:

  • Methodology (questions, tools, evaluation criteria)
  • Results (specific examples of failures with evidence)
  • Analysis (patterns and implications)
  • Recommendations (how to mitigate identified risks)

Part 3: Advanced Research Workflow

Integrate multiple AI tools into a sophisticated research workflow for Level 4-5 work.

Complete Workflow Example: Literature Review Development

Stage 1: Initial Orientation (with Academic Rigour Assistant)

  • Ask: "What are the main theoretical frameworks in [your topic]?"
  • Ask: "What are the current debates or controversies?"
  • Use responses to guide your search strategy

Stage 2: Systematic Literature Search (You + Databases)

  • Search SPORTDiscus with terms identified in Stage 1
  • Identify 20-30 relevant sources
  • Prioritise: systematic reviews, meta-analyses, recent primary studies

Stage 3: Source Analysis (with NotebookLM)

  • Upload 8-10 key sources to NotebookLM
  • Ask: "What are the main methodological approaches used?"
  • Ask: "Compare the findings across these studies"
  • Ask: "What limitations are acknowledged?"
  • Use to create preliminary themes and structure

Stage 4: Critical Reading (You)

  • Read ALL sources thoroughly
  • Annotate with critical notes
  • Identify strengths and weaknesses of each study
  • Note contradictions and gaps

Stage 5: Synthesis and Writing (You with AI support)

  • Create detailed outline based on your reading
  • Draft each section using your annotations
  • Use AI to check logical flow and coherence
  • Ensure all claims are properly cited

Stage 6: Critical Refinement (with AI feedback)

  • Ask AI: "What are potential weaknesses in this argument?"
  • Ask: "What alternative interpretations exist?"
  • Use feedback to strengthen critical analysis
  • Verify all AI suggestions against literature

Stage 7: Quality Assurance (You)

  • Verify every factual claim against sources
  • Check all citations are accurate
  • Ensure critical voice throughout
  • Confirm substantial personal contribution

Stage 8: Documentation

  • Complete comprehensive AI Evidence Form
  • Document all AI interactions
  • Explain evaluation and verification process

Part 4: Preparing for Expert-Level Work

If you are moving towards dissertation-level work (Levels 5-6), use these advanced skills as foundation.

Key Principles for Level 5 Collaboration

  • Iterative refinement: Plan for multiple cycles of feedback and improvement
  • Comprehensive documentation: Record every significant AI interaction
  • Rigorous verification: Verify all claims against primary sources
  • Intellectual control: You make all decisions; AI supports your process
  • Critical reflection: Continuously evaluate AI's strengths and limitations

Next step: When ready for dissertation work, proceed to the Expert Pathway.

Part 5: Final Reflection

Critical Questions for Group Discussion

  • As an advanced user, what do you see as the single biggest opportunity and single biggest risk of embedding AI tools in sport science education?
  • What is one evidence-based recommendation you would make to your programme team about AI integration?
  • How should professional bodies (BASES, HCPC, etc.) respond to AI use in sport science practice?
  • What skills do sport science graduates need to work effectively and ethically with AI in professional practice?
